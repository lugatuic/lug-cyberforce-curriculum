# C-Suite Briefing Prep Guide

**Last updated:** June 2026  
**Owner:** AJ  
**For:** Competitors, team captains

---

## Overview

The **C-Suite Briefing** is a recorded video (5–10 minutes) where your team explains:
1. The breach scenario (what happened)
2. Your response plan (how you'll fix it)
3. Resource needs (what you need to succeed)

It's due ~1 week after CyberForce releases the scenario. It's worth [insert percentage here] of your score.

**Key insight:** Don't panic. You have a week, and it's doable.

---

## Timeline

**Day 0:** Scenario drops (read it carefully)  
**Days 1–3:** Draft talking points, assign presenters  
**Days 3–5:** Practice, refine, record  
**Day 6:** Watch what you recorded, redo if needed  
**Day 7:** Submit  

**Pro tip:** Start early. Multiple takes are normal.

---

## What the Judges Want

**C-Suite executives** (the fictional audience) want:
- ✅ Clear explanation of the breach
- ✅ Realistic response plan
- ✅ Specific resource needs
- ✅ Professional presentation (not perfect, but not sloppy)

**They DON'T want:**
- ❌ "We found all the vulnerabilities" (you won't, and they know it)
- ❌ Technical jargon only (explain for executives, not engineers)
- ❌ Reading off a paper (sounds robotic)
- ❌ 20-minute presentation (it's 5–10 min, respect that)

## How to Prep

### **Step 1: Read the Scenario (30 min)**

When CyberForce releases the scenario:
1. Read it carefully
2. Underline key points (breach type, what was affected, etc.)
3. Understand: What's the main problem here?

**Example scenario:**
"Your company's HVAC management system was breached. Attackers installed a backdoor, exfiltrated customer data, and set up persistence."

**Key points:**
- Breach type: HVAC system + backdoor
- What was affected: Customer data
- Attacker capability: Persistence (they can come back)

### **Step 2: Draft Talking Points (1–2 hours)**

**As a team**, brainstorm answers to:

1. **What happened?** (The breach explanation)
   - How did the attackers get in? (Phishing? Unpatched system? Weak password?)
   - What did they do once inside?
   - What damage did they cause?

2. **Why did it happen?** (Root cause from a business perspective)
   - "We didn't monitor HVAC logs"
   - "Our backup had a vulnerability"
   - "We didn't have multi-factor auth"

3. **How will we fix it?** (Your plan)
   - Immediate: Kick attackers out, change passwords, secure the system
   - Short-term: Patch vulnerabilities, harden systems
   - Long-term: Better monitoring, regular updates, etc.

4. **What do we need?** (Resources)
   - Tools (monitoring, scanning, etc.)
   - Training (on new tools)
   - Time/people (to implement changes)
   - Budget (if applicable)

**Write in bullet points, not full sentences yet.**

```
Breach:
- Phishing email → employee clicked link → credentials stolen
- Attacker used credentials to access HVAC management portal
- Installed backdoor for persistence
- Exfiltrated customer database

Why it happened:
- No multi-factor auth on management portal
- Employees not trained on phishing
- HVAC system not monitored for suspicious activity
- Backup had known vulnerability

How we'll fix it:
- Immediate: Change all passwords, apply recent patches, kick out attacker
- Month 1: Deploy monitoring, enable MFA
- Month 2: Employee training on phishing
- Ongoing: Regular security audits

What we need:
- Monitoring tool (Wazuh or equivalent)
- MFA solution
- Time to train employees
- Budget for new tools (~$X)
```

### **Step 3: Assign Presenters (30 min)**

Choose 2 people (out of 4–6) to present.

**Why 2?** Each person talks for ~2.5–5 minutes. Less individual memorization = fewer reshoots.

**Who should present?**
- People comfortable speaking (not necessarily experts)
- People who can explain clearly
- Mix of 1 experienced + 1 newer person (nice modeling)

**Split the talking points:**
- Presenter 1: Breach explanation + Why it happened
- Presenter 2: How we'll fix it + What we need

---

### **Step 4: Write Full Script (1–2 hours)**

**Each presenter writes their section in full script format.**

**Example script (Presenter 1, ~2.5 min):**

```
"Hi, I'm [Name]. Here's what happened in the breach.

Three weeks ago, an employee received a phishing email that looked 
like it came from IT. They clicked a link and entered their credentials.

Using those credentials, an attacker accessed our HVAC management 
portal and installed a backdoor — basically giving them a back door 
into our system that they could use anytime.

From there, they exfiltrated our customer database. We don't know 
exactly what data was accessed, but we assume names, email addresses, 
and order history.

So the core problem is: our HVAC system wasn't protected properly. 
We had no multi-factor authentication, no monitoring, and employees 
weren't trained on phishing.

That's what happened. [Presenter 2], can you walk us through the fix?"
```

**Good practices:**
- ✅ Write like you speak (natural, not robotic)
- ✅ Explain for executives (not engineers)
- ✅ Use "we" not "I" (team effort)
- ✅ Be honest ("We don't know exactly...")
- ✅ Transition to next presenter smoothly

**Bad practices:**
- ❌ Read a dense paragraph (hard to understand)
- ❌ Use tons of jargon (they're not engineers)
- ❌ Sound rehearsed/robotic (boring)
- ❌ Claim you'll fix everything (unrealistic)

### **Step 5: Practice (1–2 hours over several days)**

**Day 3–4: Individual practice**
- Each presenter reads their section alone
- Time themselves (should be ~2.5–5 min each, 5–10 min total)
- Adjust script if words are hard to say

**Day 4–5: Team practice**
- Both presenters practice together
- Transition between speakers smoothly
- Full run-through

**Tips:**
- Use a timer (you'll go faster than you think)
- Record yourself (you'll notice umms, awkward pauses)
- Read multiple times (it gets smoother)

### **Step 6: Record (30 min to 2 hours depending on takes)**

**Setup:**
- Quiet room (no background noise, people talking)
- Good lighting (front facing, not backlit)
- Professional outfit (business casual, not t-shirt)
- Laptop/phone camera (decent quality, but doesn't need to be cinema)

**How to record:**
- Use Zoom (record locally, then upload) OR Quicktime/OBS on laptop
- Start with both presenters in frame (or alternate)
- Roll camera, let them present

**Typical outcome:** Need 2–3 takes. First take has stumbles, second is better, third is good.

**Pro tip:** Script nearby but don't read directly (have cards, cue yourself)

### **Step 7: Review & Resubmit if Needed**

Watch what you recorded:
- Does it sound natural? 
- Can you understand it?
- Is the audio clear?
- Did you cover all points?

If you hate it, record again. No shame in multiple takes.

## Common Pitfalls

**❌ Waiting until the last day to start**
→ You'll panic, script will be rushed, take a million takes

**❌ Reading directly from paper**
→ Sounds robotic, eye contact breaks, monotone

**❌ Trying to memorize everything**
→ You'll stumble, take more takes, stress

**❌ Not practicing as a team**
→ Transitions are awkward, timing is off

**❌ Dressing like you're in class**
→ You're pitching to executives, dress like you mean it

**❌ Too much jargon**
→ "We'll implement a zero-trust architecture with kernel hardening"
→ "We'll improve security by patching vulnerabilities and limiting access"

## Q&A: Common Questions

**Q: Does it have to be perfect?**  
A: No. Professional-looking yes, perfect no. If there's one small stumble but overall good presentation, submit it.

**Q: Can we have background music?**  
A: Probably not necessary. Clear audio + your voices is best.

**Q: What if someone can't make the recording day?**  
A: Reschedule or have someone else present that section. Flexibility is fine during prep.

**Q: How do we submit?**  
A: CyberForce will give you submission instructions. Usually video file + form.

**Q: Will they care about production quality?**  
A: Not really. They care about content + clarity. Phone camera is fine as long as you can hear/see clearly.

**Q: What if we disagree on the response plan?**  
A: That's okay. You all agreed on something before you started writing. Document that decision + stick with it.

## Recording Session Setup (For Team Captains)

**Book a room** (30 min, enough for 2–3 people):
- LUG room, or
- Private study room on campus, or
- Quiet meeting room

**Supplies:**
- Laptop + camera (or phone)
- Script (printed or on tablet)
- Good lighting (phone flashlight works, natural window light is great)
- Quiet environment (close door, minimize background noise)

**Check before recording:**
- Audio: Can you hear clearly? No echo?
- Video: Can you see faces? Is it in focus?
- Timing: How long did it take? (Should be 5–10 min)
