# Team Balancing Guide

**Last updated:** June 2026  
**Owner:** AJ  
**For:** Admins forming teams

---

## Overview

**Goal:** Create teams that are fairly balanced (not one team of all-stars, another of beginners).

**Why:** 
- Better learning (mixed skills = knowledge transfer)
- Better morale (teams have realistic expectations)

**How:** Use info from info session + interest forms.

---

## Data Collection

**Before forming teams, collect:**

1. **Skill assessment** (from info session or form)
   - Linux experience: Never / Basics / Comfortable / Expert
   - Windows experience: Same scale
   - Web development: Same scale
   - CTF/anomaly solving: Same scale

2. **Role preference**
   - Green team (web app)
   - Vulnerability hunting (finding issues)
   - Monitoring & hardening (defense)
   - Flexible (doesn't matter)

3. **Availability**
   - Can do 3–5 hrs/week during crunch? (Yes/No/Maybe)
   - Can make competition day? (Yes/No/Maybe)

4. **Team preference** (optional)
   - Want to be with [specific people]? (friendship groups)
   - Want to avoid [specific people]? (red flag, talk to them)

---

## Balancing Strategy

**Ideal 6-person team:**
- 1–2 high-skill people (leaders, mentors within team)
- 2–3 medium-skill people (learners + contributors)
- 1–2 lower-skill people (learning, bringing fresh perspective)

**Why this works:**
- High-skill people can lead + mentor within team
- Medium-skill people bridge gap + help lower-skill people learn
- Low-skill people are less intimidated, learn faster
- Mixed team is more resilient (if one person struggles, others pick up slack)

**Example distribution:**

| Team | Linux | Windows | Web | CTF | Vibes |
|------|-------|---------|-----|-----|-------|
| Team A | Expert | Comfortable | Comfortable | Comfortable | Mixed experience, good leaders |
| Team B | Comfortable | Comfortable | Basics | Basics | Newer folks, good learners |

Neither team is "all weak" or "all strong." Both are viable.

---

## Step-by-Step Balancing

**1. Categorize people by overall skill level**

**High-skill tier:**
- 2+ areas where they're comfortable/expert
- Has taken CS courses
- Has done programming/Linux before

**Medium-skill tier:**
- 1–2 areas where they're comfortable
- General CS knowledge
- Some technical experience

**Low-skill tier:**
- Mostly "basics" or "never"
- Willing to learn
- Eager despite inexperience

**Example:**
- High: Michael (Linux expert, CTF good), Sam (Linux + Windows comfortable)
- Medium: Alex (Linux comfortable, web basics), Jordan (CTF good, new to systems)
- Low: Casey (mostly new, willing), Taylor (basics in everything)

**2. Distribute high-skill people first**

Spread them across teams (don't put all in one team).

**3. Add medium-skill people**

Mix them in, trying to balance each team.

**4. Add low-skill people**

Pair them with high/medium-skill people when possible.

**5. Assign roles based on skills**

**6. Account for preferences**

If someone strongly prefers a role/team, honor it if possible.

**7. Check for red flags**

- **Too many new people on one team:** Rebalance (add a more experienced person)
- **No leaders:** Rebalance (assign an experienced mentor)
- **Role mismatch:** Someone who says "I like web" is on monitoring → swap roles or move people
- **Friendship cliques:** Okay if balanced, but don't put entire group together (they need to meet other people)

---

## Finalizing Teams

**Before announcing:**

1. Review each team composition
   - Does Team A have a leader? Yes ✓
   - Does Team B have someone who understands hardening? Yes ✓
   - Are skills distributed? Yes ✓

2. Confirm with team captains (who leads each team?)
   - Usually the most experienced person or someone comfortable with logistics

3. Assign mentors (if available)
   - Lower-skill team gets a mentor
   - More experienced teams might mentor themselves

4. Create team channels
   - Discord channels for team communication
   - Share roster + roles + expectations

---

## Communicating to Teams

**When you announce teams, explain:**

"We've deliberately balanced teams so each one is fair + viable. You have a mix of experienced + newer people, which is intentional:
- Experienced people: you'll mentor + lead
- Newer people: you'll learn + contribute
- Everyone: this is a team effort"

---

## Adjustments During Prep

**If teams become unbalanced during prep:**
- Someone drops out → Can we swap someone in?
- Someone emerges as leader → Confirm they're okay with it
- Team struggles → Is it skill balance or just needing mentorship?

**Be flexible.** If moving people makes sense after a few weeks, do it.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**❌ Grouping all experienced people on one team**

**❌ Creating a "newbie team" with no mentorship**
→ They get overwhelmed, drop out

**❌ Assuming people know their roles**
→ Explicitly assign roles, discuss with team

**❌ Forgetting about mentorship**
→ Assign mentors to teams that need support

**❌ Not being transparent about balancing**
→ Explain why you mixed teams (people understand fairness)

---

## Metrics for "Balanced"

A balanced team has:
- ✅ At least 1–2 experienced people (leaders)
- ✅ At least 1–2 people motivated to learn
- ✅ Mix of Linux + Windows experience (not all in one)
- ✅ Someone comfortable with web OR systems OR CTF (covers key areas)
- ✅ Clear roles assigned
- ✅ A mentor if needed
- ✅ Realistic expectations
